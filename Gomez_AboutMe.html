Annabel Gomez

Hello, my name is Annabel Gomez and I am a senior at Dublin High School. I am currently in my school's Project Lead the Way (PLTW) Engineering and Design Academy because I aspire to become and engineer (perhaps mechanical). I really enjoy inventing, innovating, and creating. I also enjoy business and leadership; I hope to also be a CEO of my own engineering firm when I am older. I appreciate when people hear my voice and ideas. Although I enjoy this, I still am open to others' ideas so that I can learn from them and create a collaborative environment. I plan to achieve my goals by taking as many PLTW classes in high school that will enhance my technical and developmental skills.

My sophomore and junior year, I was the Mechanical Lead of Dublin High School's Lemelson-MIT Inventeam that built the Special Olympics Robotic Thrower Machine, or SpORT, for short. As the Mechanical Lead, I was in charge of overseeing all c hanges made to the physical aspects of machine. I was also in charge of designing and building the turntable for the machine. In addition, I was responsible for fundraising. I was the member who raised the most money to help cover the cost of building the prototype and traveling to Boston to present it at EurekaFest at MIT. Being in this team improved my leadership and collaborative skills as I experienced the difference in productivity in our team. We went from having a lack of communication to being very inclusive and cooperative thanks to the help of our mentor.

Once the grant period ended, we could no longer call ourselves the Lemelson-MIT Inventeam team and became a subsection of the Build Club. The Build Club is my high school's main engineering academy club. I became the leader of the team continuing the maintenance and improvement of the machine we built as the Inventeam. We call ourselves the Special Olympics Team. On October 9, 2018, we presented SpORT for the first time at a local Special Olympics soccer event for elementary and middle schoolers. This was the first time that Special Olympics Participants were going to use the machine and we had to ensure that it met all of the afety regulations. At the event, my team and I could ot believe that something we had put countless hours and many long nights into had materialized into something that our community would benefit from. At first, students were intimidated by the loud motors. The first student approached the machine and, reluctantly, loaded the ball into place. With a quick motion, he hit the “Go” button and stepped back. His fear immediately disappeared and cheers erupted when the ball hit the target. Soon, other students lined up to take turns. I could tell, by how their faces lit up, that they knew that they were independently completing an otherwise difficult task. The expression of enthusiasm and genuine enjoyment, reflected on their faces, gave me a deep sense of satisfaction. It reinforced my desire to become an engineer I want my inventions to improve the lives of others. This achievement proved to me that I don't have to be an adult to make a difference!

Impacting others with my work and projects satisfies me. It's why I host workshops through the Build Club to teach my peers how to use the various machines we have at our disposal, such as 3D printers and laser cutters.
