1st Special Olympics Event - October 9, 2018

I was the Mechanical Lead of Dublin High School's Lemelson-MIT Inventeam that built the Special Olympics Robotic Thrower Machine, or SpORT, for short. As the Mechanical Lead, I was in charge of overseeing all changes made to the physical aspects of machine. Our team had an electronic documentation process. At every meeting, I would document our future plans for the machine, as well as record any changes made to the machine. I was also in charge of designing and building the turntable for the machine. I built a belt and pulley system, powered by a motor going at 3 rpm. In addition, I was responsible for fundraising. I was the member who raised the most money to cover the rest of the cost of building the prototype and traveling to Boston to present it at EurekaFest

After eighteen months of brainstorming, designing, prototyping, fundraising, presenting to our community, and planning, we finally presented our final prototype at EurekaFest at MIT. Once the grant period ended, we could no longer call ourselves the Lemelson-MIT Inventeam team and became a subsection of the Build Club. The Build Club is my high school's main engineering academy club. I became the leader of the team continuing the maintenance and improvement of the machine we had built as the Inventeam. We call ourselves the Special Olympics Team.

When SpORT came back from Boston, a few adjustments needed to be done. My team and I found out about an approaching local Special Olympics soccer event for elementary and middle schoolers in October. We got right to work, ensuring the functionality and safety of the machine for the upcoming event. This was the first time that Special Olympics Participants were going to use the machine and we had to ensure that it met all of the safety regulations.

At the event, my team and I could not believe that something we had put countless hours and many long nights into had materialized into something that our community would benefit from. The eighteen months had all come down to this. While setting up our machine, the surprised gasps from the teachers, community members, and students excited me. I could not wait for them to see what our machine could do. At first, students were intimidated by the loud motors. The first student approached the machine and, reluctantly, loaded the ball into place. With a quick motion, he hit the “Go” button and stepped back. His fear immediately disappeared and cheers erupted when the ball hit the target. Soon, other students lined up to take turns. I could tell, by how their faces lit up, that they knew that they were independently completing an otherwise difficult task. The expression of enthusiasm and genuine enjoyment, reflected on their faces, gave me a deep sense of satisfaction.

I want to become an engineer because I want to make the world a better place. I want my inventions to improve the lives of others. This achievement proved to me that I don't have to be an adult to make a difference!

These participants may not be able to play soccer, physically, but this machine gives them the opportunity to enjoy the sport more interactively!
